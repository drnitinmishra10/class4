🌿 The World of Plants

Welcome, Class 4 Students!

Plants are living things that make their own food, grow, and reproduce. They are essential for life on Earth because they provide us with oxygen, food, shelter, and medicines. In this chapter, we will explore the amazing world of plants — their parts, how they grow, different types, special adaptations, and how they make their own food!

1. Parts of a Plant

A typical plant has two main systems: the Root System (underground) and the Shoot System (above ground). Each part has a special job to do.

Diagram showing the main parts of a plant and their functions (Source: ScienceFacts.net)

Main Parts and Their Functions:

Part Function
Roots Hold the plant firmly in the soil. They absorb water and minerals from the soil.
Stem Supports the plant and holds up the leaves, flowers, and fruits. It also carries water and food between roots and leaves.
Leaves Are the "food factories" of the plant. They make food using sunlight through photosynthesis.
Flowers Are the reproductive parts. They attract insects and birds and help in making seeds and fruits.
Fruits Protect the seeds inside them. They also help in seed dispersal.
Seeds Contain the baby plant (embryo) inside. Given the right conditions, they grow into new plants.
Detailed diagram of a flower showing its internal parts (Source: SAPS)
🌸 Did You Know? The flower has male parts (stamen) and female parts (carpel). Bees and butterflies help carry pollen from one flower to another — this is called pollination!

2. How Plants Grow

Plants grow from seeds through a process called germination. A plant's life cycle shows how a tiny seed becomes a big plant and produces new seeds again.

The life cycle of a plant: from seed to adult plant (Source: ScienceFacts.net)

Stages of a Plant's Life Cycle:

  1. Seed: A seed contains a baby plant and food stored inside a protective coat.
  2. Germination: When a seed gets water, air, and warmth, it begins to grow. A tiny root grows down, and a shoot grows up.
  3. Seedling: The young plant that emerges from the soil is called a seedling. It has small leaves.
  4. Adult Plant: The seedling grows bigger, develops more leaves, stems, and roots, and becomes a mature plant.
  5. Flowering and Fruiting: The adult plant produces flowers. After pollination, flowers turn into fruits containing seeds.
  6. New Seeds: The seeds are dispersed by wind, water, animals, or humans, and the cycle begins again!
🌱 Did You Know? Some plants, like potatoes and onions, do not grow from seeds. They grow from special underground stems called bulbs and tubers!

3. Types of Plants

Scientists classify plants into different groups based on their characteristics. The main types are based on whether they have seeds, flowers, or special tissues.

The four main groups of the Plant Kingdom (Source: BioExplorer)

Main Groups of Plants:

A. Non-Flowering Plants (Plants without flowers)

  • Mosses and Liverworts: Very small plants that grow in damp places. They have no true roots, stems, or leaves.
  • Ferns: Have true roots, stems, and leaves (fronds). They reproduce using spores, not seeds.
  • Gymnosperms (Conifers): Plants with naked seeds (not inside fruits). Examples: pine, fir, and spruce trees. They have cones instead of flowers.

B. Flowering Plants (Plants with flowers)

  • Angiosperms: These are plants that produce seeds inside fruits. They are the most common plants on Earth.
    • Monocots: Plants with one seed leaf (cotyledon). Examples: wheat, rice, maize, grasses, lilies, and bananas. Their leaves have parallel veins.
    • Dicots: Plants with two seed leaves. Examples: beans, peas, roses, mango, and neem. Their leaves have net-like veins.
Feature Monocots Dicots
Seed leaves One Two
Leaf veins Parallel Net-like (reticulate)
Root system Fibrous roots Tap root
Examples Wheat, Rice, Corn, Grass Beans, Peas, Rose, Mango

4. Adaptations in Plants

An adaptation is a special feature that helps a plant survive in its environment. Different habitats have different challenges — hot deserts, cold mountains, wet ponds, and dark forests. Plants develop special body parts and behaviors to live in these places.

Desert plant adaptations: How a cactus survives in dry conditions (Source: BBC Bitesize)

Types of Adaptations:

A. Structural Adaptations (Body Parts)

  • Desert Plants (like Cactus): Have thick, fleshy stems to store water. Their leaves are reduced to spines to reduce water loss. They have deep or widespread roots to absorb water.
  • Water Plants (like Water Lily): Have floating leaves with air pockets. Their stems are flexible to move with water currents.
  • Tropical Rainforest Plants: Have large leaves to catch sunlight in dark forests. Some plants (like vines) climb on other plants to reach sunlight.
  • Tundra Plants: Grow very close to the ground to avoid strong, cold winds. They have small, waxy leaves to reduce water loss.

B. Physiological Adaptations (Internal Processes)

  • Dormancy: Some seeds sleep during bad weather and wake up when conditions are right.
  • Toxin Production: Some plants produce poisons to protect themselves from being eaten by animals.

C. Behavioral Adaptations

  • Phototropism: Plants bend towards light to get more sunlight for making food. Sunflowers follow the sun across the sky!
  • Gravitropism: Roots always grow down into the soil (positive), while shoots grow up towards the sky (negative).
🌵 Did You Know? The Venus Flytrap is a plant that eats insects! It grows in soil that lacks nutrients, so it catches and digests insects to get the nitrogen it needs.

5. How Plants Make Food

Plants are unique because they can make their own food! They do not need to eat like animals do. This process is called Photosynthesis.

The process of photosynthesis: how plants use sunlight to make food (Source: Science Sparks)

What is Photosynthesis?

Photosynthesis is the process by which plants use sunlight, water, and carbon dioxide to make their food (sugar/glucose) and release oxygen into the air.

The Photosynthesis Equation:

Carbon Dioxide + Water + Sunlight → Glucose (Sugar) + Oxygen
(in the presence of Chlorophyll)

Key Requirements for Photosynthesis:

Requirement Where it comes from Role
Sunlight The Sun Provides energy for the process
Water Absorbed by roots from soil Used as a raw material
Carbon Dioxide Taken from air through tiny holes in leaves (stomata) Used as a raw material
Chlorophyll Green pigment in leaves Traps sunlight energy

How it Works:

  1. Leaves have a green pigment called chlorophyll inside tiny structures called chloroplasts.
  2. Chlorophyll traps sunlight energy.
  3. Water travels up from the roots through the stem to the leaves.
  4. Carbon dioxide enters the leaf through small openings called stomata.
  5. Using sunlight energy, the plant converts water and carbon dioxide into glucose (a type of sugar — the plant's food).
  6. Oxygen is released as a by-product through the stomata. This oxygen is what we breathe!
🍃 Did You Know? The oxygen released by plants during photosynthesis is the reason we can breathe! One large tree can produce enough oxygen in a day for two people. That's why we must protect our forests!
